Background Information

What are particles?

Subatomic particles, such as protons, neutrons, electrons, muons, neutrinos, quarks and mesons make up all matter (and antimatter). The smallest individual particles are called fundamental particles. Small particles like these make up protons and neutrons.

What are decays?

Many particles are unstable, meaning that they decay into other particles after they are produced. The particles decay into less massive particles (particles with smaller masses). Particles have different lifetimes, or the typical amount of time it takes them to decay. These lifetimes can be as small as 5×10-25 seconds,[1]the average lifetime of the top quark... or the particle could have a very long lifetime. Neutrons, for example, have a lifetime of almost 15 minutes.[2]

How are particles detected?

Wire chambers, such as the Central Outer Tracker are simulated in our particle tracks website. As the charged particle moves in a circular path (due to the magnetic field), wires sense the charge of the particle, and the path of the particle can be reconstructed. Physicists can then calculate the momentum of the particles using the trajectories and identify them. Particles create smaller or larger curvature of path depending on their momentums.
There are many other ingenious ways physicists detect and visualize subatomic particles. The first detector invented was cloud chambers. Other devices include: Cloud Chamber, Bubble Chamber, and the Large Hadron Collider.

What is the momentum and energy conservation?

Energy is defined by the equation E = (p2 + m2)1/2 with p as the momentum of the particle, and m as the mass.[3]Momentum can be found with the equation: p = 0.299792 B r, where B is the strength of the magnetic field, and r is the radius of the circle the particles create. Energy and momentum are conserved.
